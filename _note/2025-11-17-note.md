We crave to understand the world.  
Understanding seems to begin with a question and end with an answer.  
But where does a question come from?

Perhaps from some objective.  
You need to understand B in order to achieve A.  
When there is no such objective in sight, we can call that a purely epistemic desire for understanding.  
But what is the nature of such a desire?

It appears to be a desire for a certain kind of completion within one’s world model. And a world model is contingent on one’s sensory and motor system—on what you can perceive and how you can act in the world. All in all, any question worthy of understanding arises from a particular perspective acquired through nature and nurture.

So in a sense, it is the world that gives rise to a question in an epistemic being.

The process of gaining understanding is often thought of as a creative cognitive process. But the rise of questions is another process, just as interesting and mysterious as the cognitive one.

What I am doing here is trying to make this question-making, question-answering loop itself an object of understanding.  
And I want to argue that modern AI participates in only a part of this loop.
